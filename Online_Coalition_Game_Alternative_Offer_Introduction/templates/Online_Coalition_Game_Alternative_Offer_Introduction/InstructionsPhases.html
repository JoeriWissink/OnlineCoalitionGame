We will now explain how negotiations will proceed. As mentioned before, you will have to decide with whom you want to form a coalition and how you want to distribute the available budget. The other negotiators face the same decisions. Moreover, a coalition is only formed when all members of a proposed coalition agree on how to distribute the budget. For this reason, negotiations may take one or multiple rounds. Every round consists of 3 phases.

Phase I: Making offers

To start negotiations, in Phase I each negotiator makes an offer. An offer consists of: 1) an offer on which coalition you want to form, 2) an offer on how you want the budget to be distributed within this coalition; how much do you want for yourself and how much do you want the other to get?

Note that you can only make offers in which you are involved yourself. For example, the negotiator for party A can only offer to form an AB-coalition (coalition between party A and party B) or an AC-coalition (coalition between party A and C). Because A is not involved in a BC-coalition (coalition between party B and party C), A cannot propose this coalition.

Phase II: Choosing offers

After all negotiators made an offer, all negotiators will see all offers. These are the offers that they have made themselves, and the offers that the other parties made. Next, every negotiator chooses the offer with the coalition and budget distribution that they want to execute. This can be an offer that another party made, but also the offer they made themselves.

Note that you can only choose offers in which you are involved yourself. For example, the negotiator for party B can only offer to form an AB-coalition (coalition between party A and party B) or a BC-coalition (coalition between party B and C). Because B is not involved in an AC-coalition (coalition between party A and party C), B cannot choose this coalition.

Phase III: Seeing chosen offers

In Phase III, all negotiators receive feedback about which offers are chosen by whom. If two negotiators have selected the same offer, a tentative coalition is formed.

Of course, it is also possible that no offer is selected by two negotiators. In this case, no tentative coalition is formed and a new bargaining round starts. All parties will then return to Phase I and make a new offer.

Phase IV: Making an alternative offer

In Phase IV, the negotiator not included in the tentative offer makes an alternative offer to one of the negotiators in the tentative coalition.

Phase V: Ratyfing the tentative coalition or accepting the alternative offer

In Phase V, the negotiators in the tentative coalition are asked whether they want to ratify the tentative coalition. If all negotiators in the tentative coalition choose to do this, the tentative coalition becomes the final coalition and the budget is allocated as agreed upon. If, however, the recipient of the alternative offer chooses this alternative offer, the alternative offer becomes the new tentative coalition and a new alternative offer will be made by the one not in the new tentative coaliton.
